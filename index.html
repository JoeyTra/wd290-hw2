Overview

In 1874, a group of young painters in Paris staged a quiet rebellion against academic art by organizing their own exhibition outside the official Paris Salon. Artists such as Claude Monet rejected polished, historical scenes in favor of quick brushstrokes, visible texture, and modern subjects—cafés, city streets, and changing light. Critics mocked the style as unfinished and derisively labeled it Impressionism, but the name stuck, capturing the artists’ goal of painting fleeting visual impressions rather than idealized reality.

“Art is not what you see, but what you make others see.” — Edgar Degas

By the early 20th century, Impressionism had transformed the direction of modern art. Its emphasis on perception, color, and individual vision inspired later movements like Post-Impressionism, Fauvism, and even abstraction. More broadly, Impressionism helped legitimize artistic experimentation, breaking the authority of academic institutions and opening the door for artists to redefine what art could be in the modern world.

National Gallery of Art, Washington, D.C, Artsy
